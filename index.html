1. Intro


Learning isn't a skill often explicitly taught in school. So, people often think, "I'm just not smart enough," or "I can't remember things as well as that person - they must be gifted." And hey, I'm not going to lie, there's some truth to that. Factors like your educational background, your upbringing, how confident you are, and even a natural inclination towards certain topics can play a big role in your ability to learn. But I truly believe there's a way for everyone to boost their productivity when learning something new. It's all about understanding your personal learning cycles, especially when you're immersing yourself in expansive fields of knowledge, such as language acquisition or programming.

Most people can relate to the feeling of being hyper-fixated on a topic, diving into it, learning a bunch, but then getting burnt out. That's when those Twitter gurus tell you it's about consistency and discipline. But wouldn't it be nice if we could stay motivated almost every day? üßê

2. Discovery


The theory of having a learning cycle definitely exists (if you're interested in that you can simply google "learning cycle"), but I only figured out mine after I began learning Mandarin and coding. I routinely get people telling me "you must be a fast learner!" or asking questions like "how do you plan your studies?". It got me thinking - what am I doing differently? So I decided to share my process.

*disclaimer: I can't guarentee this will work for everyone, but it's what works best for me üòÑ

With no previous experience learning Mandarin, and after studying for only about a year, I achieved a relatively high level of fluency compared to my peers. But why? what am I doing differently that other people are not? same with coding, although I'm not sure how my speed compares to others, I still would like to share my process. Even if it helps just one other person I would be happy. üòÑ

3. Process


So here's the gist: I go through three main cycles when studying. Being aware of which cycle I'm in at any given moment has helped me stay productive without burning out. But keep in mind, for this process to work best, you have to actually enjoy what you're learning about. If it works for subjects you don't like as much, that's great, but no guarantees!

CYCLE 1

structure

This cycle is all about learning through a clear-cut, structured path.

If you're a developer, think of the times you've spent learning on platforms like freeCodeCamp, Scrimba, or Codecademy. In regard to learning a language, this could be attending a language course, or following some kind of predeterimined learning path, like in a textbook. At this point, you will be getting your head around the fundamental concepts that are crucial to the subject you're learning.

This structure acted as a sort of roadmap. I never felt lost or overwhelmed because I knew exactly where I was and what I was supposed to do next. The concrete tasks provided me with clear goals, and achieving them gave me a sense of accomplishment and motivation.

This is essentially what the 'Structure' phase in the learning cycle is about. It's a guided, step-by-step journey where you're progressively introduced to core principles. It's a controlled environment where you learn, practice, make mistakes, and improve. But just like anything, after a while, things can start to feel a bit repetitive and you may find youself getting bored or burnt out.

This phase is pretty straightforward, and when you're new to the subject, you'll probably hang out here for a good while. Depending on how many hours you spend a day learning you could be here for a few days up to a few weeks.

How do you know when to move on to the next cycle? well, when you are feeling bored and totally demotivated. Feeling like your productivity has really decreased.

Then you move onto CYCLE 2

CYCLE 2

explore

The main purpose of this cycle is to inspire a new sense of motivation within yourself.

Imagine you have been in cycle 1 studying JavaScript for a week and feel totally tired. Trying to reignite your motivation and energy from the very thing that has burned you out can seem like an uphill battle. This is where understanding the concept of the 'puppy mind' can help.

The "puppy mind" describes the tendency for the mind to get easily distracted and jump from one thing to another, much like a playful puppy. This can lead to difficulty in focusing on one task for an extended period of time.

People mostly agree that we need to train/discipline the puppy mind, and while I agree, there are some situations (like in cycle 2) that it might be beneficial to let your puppy mind run rampant.

The point of this cycle is to get a fresh perspective on learning, maximize your input sources to stimulate creativity.

The explore cycle would generally only last for a few hours or maximum 1 to 2 days depending on your learning schedule, and how burnt out you let yourself get from CYCLE 1.

All of these new sources of input can get you inspired and ready for CYCLE 3. Ideally CYCLE 3 should start after CYCLE 2 has resulted in a good idea, otherwise feel free to move back to CYCLE 1. But trust me, if you've done CYCLE 2 right, you'll be ready for CYCLE 3 every single time.

CYCLE 3

create

This is where your ideas come to life through projects.

After the exploration phase, hopefully, you have come up with an idea for your CYCLE 3 project.

The idea for a project could surface from realizing a problem that needs solving or drawing inspiration from something that piqued your interest. I have been inspired to make a project from all sorts of places, like a challenge on front-end mentor, realizing my mom needed a new website, or one of the assigned projects on freeCodeCamp. It doesn't matter if you think you're a creative person or not, there are plenty of resources to get inspired and make a project.

Once you are finished with your project you can move back to CYCLE 1 and learn more fundamentals and new concepts.

Don't be afraid to switch learning resources if something is not clicking with you, and make things that you are passionate about. If I am doing something boring I can barely concentrate for 30 minutes, but if I'm passionate, I can go for hours on end!
